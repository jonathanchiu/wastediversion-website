Introduction

Waste Diversion LLC started off as a university research project named Waste Diversion DC. It was a Clinton Global Initiative University 2012 project that was conceived by two George Washington University graduate students Kholood Altassan, MBBS, MPH, and Poonam Sandhu, RN, BSN, MPH. They were assisted by advisor and Professor of Environmental and Occupational Health and of Epidemiology and Biostatistics, Katherine Hunting PhD, MPH.

Waste Diversion's main focus at the time was to collect evidence that would support its efforts to encourage District of Columbia Council to change the special event permitting process, such that waste diversion would become a mandatory component of all permitted Special Events.

The above focus was recently fully realized! Click here to find out more!

Objectives

The university project had three research objectives:

  1. Would providing waste diversion infrastructure (recycling and composting) at public events influence the behaviors of attendees towards waste disposal?
  2. How much waste would be diverted from the landfills by providing waste diversion infrastructure at a public event in D.C.?
  3. Comparing the three different types of waste diversion stations provided at a public event — one staffed and labeled, one labeled, one neither staffed nor labeled — which would result in the most waste diversion?

Methods

On October 7, 2012, Waste Diversion DC ran a randomized controlled study at "Taste of DC," a 100,000 person-strong annual food event. Thirty-one low-income workers from Street Sense DC were employed and trained for its project. It also enlisted the services of the DC Department of Public Works and 25 volunteers. Three different types of waste diversion stations were placed throughout three blocks of the event:

  • 20 stations labeled and staffed with trained volunteers who instructed attendees on proper recycling and composting materials (Intervention)
  • 10 stations only labeled with instructions on proper disposal of waste materials
  • 10 stations with neither staff nor labels (Control)

Full bags of trash, recycling, and composting were inspected for contamination by other categories of improperly disposed waste materials. The bags were then weighed by WDDC trained volunteers. Using bag weight and number of bags, proportions of recycling and composting to trash were calculated for each type of station. Thus, an evaluation was performed on the waste diverting effects of the different stations. Contamination was also examined to assess efficient sorting of the waste stream.

Results

Over a period of 3 hours we were able to divert 196 lbs in recyclable and compostable materials from a total 542 pounds of waste. Our analysis yielded the following:

  • By weight: over 40% of the waste collected from our intervention stations were recyclable and compostable materials versus only 21% in our controlstations (Figure 1).
  • By number of bags: the intervention stations increased recycling by 2.4 times butshowed no effect on composting (Figure 2).
  • Using a Pearsonâ€™s chi-square we evaluated the previously described association with number of bags diverted, recycling and composting combined, and number of bags of trash and found it to be significant with a p-value of 0.053.
  • Contamination of the waste stream was evaluated and the intervention stations were found to have lower levels of contamination than the controls (Table 1 & 2).
  • The contamination assessment was found to be significantly biased by the inspectors when looking acrossthe two teams, with a p-value 0f 0.009.

Discussion

This project was the first attempt to introduce waste diversion to a public event in Washington D.C. The project faced a few obstacles which limited our findings.

  • First, although we randomized the placement of our stations, taking into account areas of increased traffic, certain stations received more trash than others.
  • Secondly a number of food vendors moved the composting and recycling bins, resulting in a smaller number of stations we could collect data from and also eliminated the labeled stations from the analysis due to lack of data.
  • Although it initially appeared that the intervention stations were more efficient at sorting the waste with lower contamination levels, thisfinding wassignificantly biased by ourinspectors.
  • We also faced rain and unexpected cold temperatures, which altered the anticipated attendance rates at the event and the consequent trash disposal trends. Additionally, although care was taken to insure that the bins were covered, the rain may have leaked in, adding water weight to our bags.

Conclusions

Even given its challenges, the outcome of the intervention was clear; event patrons will use waste diversion stations if they are provided. When these waste stations are staffed, providing awareness and knowledge to attendees, waste diversion rates increase.

The above information was presented to DC Council in the form of a policy recommendation. Based upon our evidence and background information, on June 18th Councilmember Mary Cheh introduced a bill that will mandate recycling at outdoor events in Washington, DC. The bill will now go through the usual process of a public hearing, two council votes, clearance through congress and then a signature by the DC mayor for final approval.
